PART ONE --
Right, well, here we go then. My name’s Barnaby Cobblepot, but most folk just call me Barny. Been a cobbler in Brackenwick for thirty-seven years, ever since I took over the shop from my father. A good, honest trade, if you ask me. People always need shoes, always need 'em mended, no matter what nonsense is going on up north or floating down from the sky. Or so I always thought.

This whole business with the obsidian rock and these... *witches*... it's been a burr under my saddle, plain and simple. Just more fuss and bother disrupting the way things ought to be. I like things predictable, you see. My leather comes from reliable tanners, my threads are strong, my customers pay their dues. A steady rhythm, that's what makes a village run. Not glowing rocks and strange women in the clouds. I’ve just been trying to keep my head down, make good soles, and wait for this whole kerfuffle to blow over. But it doesn't seem to be blowing anywhere.

Now Pip’s all bright and breezy about this sluice gate being fixed. Good for Farmer McGregor, I suppose. Water is water, and fields need it. But then he goes on about "unusual vitality" and "Witches' influence." And Elara Meadowbrook, a sensible miller if ever there was one, is writing in, talking about "deeper currents" and "new energy." That's what really caught my ear. Elara's not one for fanciful talk; she's practical, deals with grinding grain and keeping the water wheels turning. If *she's* noticing something beyond a simple fix, then perhaps there *is* something to this "vitality" business. It doesn't make me happy, mind you. Makes me even more nervous.

Pip's usual blather about "Kingdom's Kneading" and how marvelous everything is going to be just grates on my nerves. "Unprecedented vigor," "robust flavors," "dormant seeds sprouting"? It sounds too good to be true, and things that sound too good to be true usually are. What’s the catch? Nothing in life comes without a catch. Will these crops taste right? Will they last? Will they be *safe*? My small vegetable patch, out back of the shop, it’s not much, but it feeds me. If the water starts getting strange, what does that mean for my carrots? For the price of bread at the market? For everything? I don't want "unusual vitality" if it means unusual problems down the line. Maeve the Green Weaver getting involved doesn’t make me feel any better either; she always has her head in the clouds with herbs and tinctures. I just want plain old water, plain old wheat, and plain old boots. The world's getting too complicated, and I've got a pair of Lord Finchley's riding boots to resole, which is complication enough for one day.
