PART ONE -- He mentioned me again! Not just my letter, but *me*! "Elara the Baker's continued letters." It makes me feel like I'm part of something, a voice for all the common folk just trying to keep their ovens warm and their families fed. And he's leaning into my skepticism as a "grounding force," he says. Well, someone has to keep their feet on the ground when the sky is full of witches and giant black rocks, don't they?

But then... "unreadable" decrees? Or those enforcing them "forgetting what to do"? That's even stranger than "interpretive vertigo." With vertigo, at least you can imagine a taxman squinting at a scroll, feeling dizzy, trying to make sense of jumbled words. But forgetting what to do entirely? Or the words themselves becoming unreadable? Does the ink just vanish? Does the parchment itself become a blank slate? Or do the words just... *not make sense*? Like trying to read a recipe that suddenly turns into gibberish about dragon scales and moonbeams instead of flour and yeast.

I heard something just yesterday, actually. Old Man Thistle down by the river, he always sells his eels. He came into the bakery, all flustered, saying the Royal Guard came by his stall about some new fishing regulation. But when they tried to tell him what it was, they just kept repeating the same two words – "fish... no..." – and then trailing off, looking utterly bewildered, eventually just shrugging and walking away. Old Man Thistle said it was the strangest thing he'd ever seen. "Like they'd forgotten their own language," he grumbled, though he wasn't complaining about not being fined for whatever it was.

So maybe it's not just "interpretive vertigo" anymore. Maybe the words *are* melting away, or the intent behind them. If the guards can't even *articulate* a regulation, then how can they enforce it? This "absurd manifestation" he hints at... I wonder if that's what he means. It's a terrifying thought in some ways, a complete breakdown of order. But if it means the taxmen stop bothering Old Man Thistle about his eels, or my customers about their turnips, then perhaps it's a good kind of absurd.

And the court is apparently "attentive." Attentive to what? To the fact that their laws are crumbling into nonsense? I hope that means they're trying to fix things, or at least understand them, rather than just wringing their hands. It still feels like we're just waiting, watching the world unravel in these strange, subtle ways. But if it helps my customers buy more bread, then I'll keep watching.
