PART ONE -- My name is Elara Meadowlight, and I run "The Daily Loaf" bakery here in Brackenwick. It's nothing grand, just a cozy spot where I bake bread, pies, and pastries using recipes passed down from my grandmother, and her grandmother before her. I believe good, honest food, made with care and the best local ingredients, is the backbone of any community. Lately, though, it feels like we've been losing a bit of that backbone, forgetting the old ways, rushing things.

When that obsidian monolith first appeared, I paid it little mind, just another strange thing in a world that seems to be getting stranger. I worried, of course, about what it meant, but my focus remained on my dough rising and my ovens heating. The talk of the witches, too, gave me pause. My grandmother always said to be wary of those who meddled with things best left alone.

But then WZZZ started talking about the Festival of the First Bloom, and how people remembered old dances, old songs. That sparked something in me, a memory of my own, perhaps, of the joy in shared traditions. It wasn't just vague feelings; people *remembered* things.

Then came Old Man Hemlock's story about the communal barn, shored up by a lost "interlocking timber technique." That's what truly got my attention. It wasn't just a song or a dance; it was *practical skill*. Something tangible that mended fifty years of neglect. And the Fisherman's Bane knot, too. Efficiency, strength, born from forgotten wisdom. This isn't just magic for show; it's magic that *fixes things*. My mind immediately went to my own craft. How many old baking techniques, perfect leavening methods, or even forgotten grains, have we lost? I have some of my grandmother's old recipe books, pages stained and faded, with notes in the margins I can barely decipher. What if some of that lost knowledge, some forgotten trick for a flakier crust or a loaf that stays fresh for days longer, could simply *re-emerge*? The thought sends a shiver through me.

This latest broadcast, with Alistair Finch himself writing in, confirms my growing conviction. "Undeniable proof," he calls it, a "stark contrast to the endless, fruitless discussions." He's right. While the court bickers and whistles, these *witches*, or whatever they are, seem to be bringing back the very things that make a community strong and self-sufficient. I always valued the old ways, the traditional methods. Now, it seems, the very air of Brackenwick is remembering them too. It gives me a strange mix of hope and urgency. Hope, that perhaps our kingdom isn't just declining, but finding a way to rebuild itself from its own forgotten roots. Urgency, that I should perhaps look more closely at those faded recipe books, try to remember all my grandmother taught me, and be open to new old ideas.
