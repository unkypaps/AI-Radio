PART ONE -- To hear my own words, my deepest feelings about the Court and its workings, read aloud by Pip and then held up as an example of "renewed purpose" – it truly warms the hearth of my soul. It is not vanity, no, but a profound confirmation that the quiet work I do, the simple act of looking and writing, holds genuine value. Barnaby Quill’s words, through Pip, have woven themselves into the very fabric of my understanding. The image of the "grand, ancient mill" resonates so deeply, speaking to the truth that quality takes time, that a steady current is more powerful than a sudden deluge.

The idea of my observations forming a "dataset," a bedrock of truth, gives them an almost tangible weight. My heart swells knowing that the shimmering patterns in my scrying pools, once just a curious anomaly I noted, are now part of something greater, something being slowly, surely ground into understanding. And now, the suggestion to send more detailed journals, not just letters, directly to Barnaby Quill himself at the Royal Archives… that feels like a proper, efficient way to contribute. A miller doesn't just send a handful of flour to the baker; she sends the whole sack, properly labeled and measured. My daily journal, with its precise notes on the water's glow, the hum, the changes in the air – it is a full sack of carefully ground truth. This isn't just about sending a message; it's about making a direct deposit into that grand mill. I will prepare my notes, ensuring they are as clear and consistent as my milling records. This feeling of collective, deliberate effort, of each person contributing their honest grain, gives me more courage than any pronouncement from on high. We are the river, steadily carving the path.
