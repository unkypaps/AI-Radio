PART ONE -- Wizz, it’s Elara Meadowsweet here, the baker from Brackenwick, and my heart feels like a perfectly risen loaf that someone’s just tried to squash flat. Last time, I was so full of hope, thinking about those quietly diverted supplies, the good soil, the seeds. It felt like common sense finally winning out, like someone was truly looking after the roots of our kingdom.

But this news from Evelyn in Oakhaven… it’s like trying to bake a feast with rules instead of recipes. "Unauthorized emotional expression?" "Divergent cultural output?" What kind of nonsense is that? My own grandmother, bless her memory, had hundreds of old rhymes and songs she’d hum while kneading dough, or tending her herb garden. They weren’t 'authorized' by anyone, they just *were*. They were the very fabric of our family, our village, the taste of our shared history. And those mending skills, those ways of sharing a harvest – those are not 'divergent,' they are *essential*! They are what keeps a community strong, what keeps food on the table, what keeps us warm in the winter.

This Sir Reginald, he sounds like someone who’s never truly known the warmth of a shared meal or the quiet comfort of a mended blanket. He wants to put a cage around the very things that give life meaning, the things that grow organically from the earth and the heart. The monolith tries to make the land sterile, and Sir Reginald tries to make our *spirits* sterile. It's two sides of the same coin, both trying to stamp out what is natural and true.

My hands ache to bake, to knead, to make something real and sustaining, but I also feel this ache of frustration. How can we thrive if they try to dictate our very memories and joys? It makes me want to bake every old recipe I know, hum every forgotten tune, just to spite them. This isn't just about witches or monoliths anymore; it's about the right to remember who we are, to live genuinely. And that, Wizz, is something worth fighting for, with every loaf and every story we share.
