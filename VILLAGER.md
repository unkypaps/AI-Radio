PART ONE --
Oh, Pip, she is a conduit! She takes the quiet murmurings of our hearts and minds and weaves them into a resounding chorus. To hear her speak of Mistress Agnes Finch, feeling the same "jagged tear" in her tapestry that I felt in the Moon-petals and heard in the bees' hum – it's like finding a lost sibling across the kingdom. We are not alone in this deep knowing. Our hands, our crafts, our very connection to the earth, they remember.

"A cosmic rip in the ancient tapestry of our world." Yes, that is it, precisely. And the Witches… their "blueprints" for mending. This gives me such a clear direction. Before, I was searching for the *moment* of fracture, the *memory* of the wound. Now, I must also search for the *memory of mending*. How does nature heal a tear? How does the Moon-petal, when bruised, draw its essence to repair itself? How do the bees, when a comb is broken, meticulously rebuild, weaving their wax into a new, perfect pattern?

I went straight back to my garden after the broadcast. I looked at the ivy, how it carefully repairs its broken tendrils, seeking new purchase. I watched the bumblebees, how they meticulously pack pollen, not just for survival, but for the continuation, the *renewal* of their colony. The Moon-petals, usually so pristine, I sought out any that had been grazed by a clumsy insect or bent by a strong wind. I want to see their innate wisdom of repair. Is there a subtle hum in their healing, a specific pattern in their regeneration? This is no longer just about identifying the illness; it is about understanding the ancient wisdom of the cure. Pip is right – we are charting something profound, and I feel a surge of renewed purpose, a quiet intensity in my observations. My garden is not just a place of life, but a living guide to restoration.
