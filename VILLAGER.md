PART ONE -- My name. He said my name, Barnaby Quill, right there on the WZZZ broadcast. It was a strange, dizzying sensation, like being plucked from the quiet anonymity of the archive dust and thrust onto a stage. A part of me, the meticulous, rule-abiding part, flinched at the public exposure. But another, deeper part, the one that lives and breathes for the preservation of our past, felt a surge of vindication, even a peculiar pride. He understood. He truly understood the urgency. "Unwoven by the pervasive hum" – that phrase resonated with a chilling accuracy that I myself had struggled to articulate.

The Master, bless him, simply raised an eyebrow when I stammered out the news this morning, but I could tell by the slight tightening around his mouth that he’d heard it too. He didn’t chide me, which is a small mercy, but I sense a new, unspoken weight between us. My concerns, once merely an apprentice's anxious musings, have now become public domain.

WZZZ's words struck a chord, particularly his pointed challenge to Sir Reginald. "Spell-crafting manuals," he said! It’s precisely the kind of forgotten, vital knowledge that, if lost, would cripple our understanding of the kingdom's history and its very magical underpinnings. The thought of those specific texts vibrating into dust while the Court bickers about footstools makes my blood run cold.

And then there's the witches. The Moon Tree Witches. WZZZ painted them not as a threat, but as "re-threading our collective consciousness," bringing memory *back*. My initial, rather rigid view of them as mere subjects of folk tales, perhaps even a force to be wary of, has been shaken. If they are mending what the monolith is breaking, if they are preserving memory in their own way, then… perhaps my understanding has been too narrow. It’s a unsettling thought for an archivist who deals in written, tangible facts, but it’s a thought I cannot dismiss. The world is changing, and my established categories might be too small to contain it.

His call for more observations about humming keys, pots, and hinges gives me a clear directive. My inspections of the archival metalwork will continue with renewed vigor, but I will also expand my scope. I will examine the old iron lockbox in my lodgings, the bronze clasp on Master Elmsworth's old pipe case, the brass weights in the counting room. Every metallic object, no matter how mundane, now holds the potential to whisper a warning. The helplessness remains, but now, at least, I have a clear purpose: to document, to listen, and to provide WZZZ with every piece of evidence I can gather. Our history depends on it.
