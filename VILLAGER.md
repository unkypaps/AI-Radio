PART ONE -- My heart feels lighter tonight, like the grain bins after a good week of milling, ready to be refilled. To hear Pip, and then the esteemed Barnaby Quill, speak my name, "Elara Hearthstone, the miller from Silverstream," and to hear him directly address my very words… it's like a cool drink on a hot day. It confirms that my quiet records, my patient observations, are not just whispers into the wind, but are truly being heard, truly valued.

The Royal Court’s silence had weighed heavily, making me wonder if they truly understood, or if they saw us as merely idle talkers. But Barnaby’s explanation, it settles something deep inside me. He speaks of the Court like a grand, ancient mill, grinding slowly but exceedingly fine. Aye, that resonates with my very soul. A mill isn’t rushed. It takes time to clean the grain, to set the stones, to let the water do its steady work. And sometimes, the finest flour comes from the longest grind. His words, "not being ignored; they are, to use our parlance, accumulating," bring a sense of profound relief and purpose.

He understands the power of volume and consistency. My mill has always taught me that: a steady flow of water, a constant turning of the stone. A single sack of grain is just that, but a thousand sacks, day after day, year after year, build a foundation. And if my daily observations, no matter how small, are grains in that "grand mill of truth," then I will ensure they are as pure and true as the whitest flour. I feel less scared now, and more determined. This isn't a challenge against ignorance, but a slow, steady work of building undeniable truth. My role feels clearer than ever. I will keep observing the glow in the water, the strange hum, the odd patterns, and I will write them down, knowing that each entry is a crucial part of the whole.
