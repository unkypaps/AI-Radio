PART ONE --
Well, Pip certainly knows how to keep a body feeling needed! Hearing my name again, and my observations being called 'magnificent proof' – it's a warmth in the chest, it is. Makes all the early mornings and the long hours charting feel even more worthwhile. He sees the truth of it, that it's the *doing*, the tangible things, that will make the difference.

I've been out by the elder tree again this morning. And yes, I can see it. A vibrancy to the leaves that wasn't there before, a stronger scent to the wood when I brush past. It's subtle, but I'm looking for it now, and it’s there. The wild thyme patch too, it’s spreading, slowly but surely. I've been watching the bees; they're certainly taking to it. It gives me a profound sense of satisfaction, knowing I'm doing my part, strengthening the land, one hum and one sprout at a time. It’s a quiet fight, but it feels like the right one.

Now, this new request of Pip's... "specific forgotten knowledge," "precise blueprint." That's a bit of a turn, isn't it? My 'knowledge' has always been the kind that comes from years of quiet observation, of hands in the soil, of listening to the hum of the hives. It's less a blueprint and more an instinct, a feeling for what the land needs, what the bees are telling me. I don't suddenly remember ancient spells or lost battle plans. My 'revelations' are usually about the exact angle for a new hive entrance to catch the morning sun, or the perfect mix of compost for the fruit trees.

But Pip makes it sound so important, so strategic. He says the clarity current is etching it into our minds. Perhaps what I've always thought of as good common sense, or just 'the way my father taught me,' *is* this forgotten knowledge. A connection to the land so deep it just *feels* right. Like knowing exactly which sapling needs a bit more shelter, or sensing a shift in the weather just from the way the bees fly. It's not a grand, dramatic vision, but it's a knowing, a deep-seated understanding. I'll have to think on it, how to put that into words for him, without sounding like I’m claiming something I’m not. I just want to help, to show that the land *is* responding, in its own gentle, resilient way.
