PART ONE --
Well, I’ll be hammered and heeled! Pip read my letter *again*. My very words, "Barnaby Cobblepot, the esteemed cobbler from Oakhaven," over the airwaves. It gives a man a funny feeling, like he’s shouting across a valley and someone actually shouts back. And that someone is Pip, who sounds like he’s genuinely buoyed by it. It’s... good. It’s good to feel heard, to know that my small efforts are not just keeping my own head above water, but perhaps helping others, or at least strengthening Pip’s resolve.

And he’s right, bless his earnest heart. Writing things down *does* help. The little notes I’ve been making, the daily record of thoughts, the careful sketching of designs before they flit away – it’s a tangible barrier against this creeping fuzziness. My workbench is a bit messier with all the scraps of parchment, but my mind feels sturdier. And those mental exercises, the quiet focus I’ve been applying while I stitch or tap in a sole, they’re like the careful tightening of a lace; they hold things together. It’s not magic, it’s just... good, honest work for the mind, same as for the hands.

The best part, though, the *best* part, is that Pip is actually going to bring Maeve, the Green Weaver, on to talk about practical, physical methods. That’s exactly what I asked for! Not some airy-fairy mumbo jumbo, but solid, useful advice a working man can apply. Things I can *do* to keep my wits sharp. My Aetherial Anchor is still the goal, the big, long-term fix I’m saving for, but these immediate actions, these daily fights against the fog, are vital. And if Maeve can offer something truly *tangible*, something I can incorporate into my daily routine without turning my workshop into a magic parlour, then I’m all ears. More than all ears, I’m practically a sieve, ready to soak up whatever practical wisdom she has.

"Steadfast hammer strokes," he called them, my words. It’s a bit much, maybe, but it makes me feel like my small contribution matters. Like I'm not just a lone cobbler in Oakhaven, but part of something larger. And that, in itself, feels like a little bit of clarity in these foggy times.
