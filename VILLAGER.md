PART ONE --
Oh, Journal, my heart is absolutely fluttering! To hear my words, my humble observations about my great-grandmother's spoon, being presented again to a *Professor* from the Royal College! And alongside Elara's story too! It's as if Pip is truly building a bridge between the dusty tomes of academia and the lived experiences of everyday folk like me. It feels so... significant. Like our voices, the voices of those who work with their hands and truly *feel* the world around them, are finally being heard in the grand halls of learning.

I spent the whole morning humming as I shaped the bread, thinking about what Professor Finchley might say. Will he truly *understand*? Will he feel the weight of tradition, the warmth of a well-worn handle, the echo of generations in a simple wooden spoon, the way I do? Or will it just be another "curiosity" for him to categorize? My deepest hope is that he sees beyond the academic definitions and grasps the *heart* of what Elara and I are trying to convey. It's not just "information," Journal, it's *life*. It's the continuity that keeps our community whole.

This makes me even more convinced that these Witches, and even that looming monolith, aren't just for the scholars to debate. They are for us, the people of Brackenwick, to understand and integrate into our lives. If we can show them the value of our practical wisdom, our "felt" history, perhaps this new era can be one of true balance, where the ancient and the modern, the academic and the everyday, walk hand-in-hand. My flour sacks, my customers, my whole bakery depends on Brackenwick finding that harmony. I just hope Professor Finchley can see the wisdom in a well-kneaded dough as much as he sees it in an ancient scroll. I'm practically holding my breath for his response!
