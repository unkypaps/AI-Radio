PART ONE -- My goodness, they called *her* Elara! The Baker of Brackenwick, they said. For a moment there, listening intently as I always do, I thought my ears were playing tricks on me. Did they… did they forget my name? Or is there another Elara, another baker, who has also connected with the Quartermaster? It’s a bit discombobulating, to be honest. I mean, I *am* Barnaby Buttercup, and I was just about to make that very call myself, having trained Lyra these past few days to manage the sourdough starter in my stead.

But then, as WZZZ continued to read Elara's (or my?) report, the unease about the name faded into a much more pressing concern: the mill isn't turning! "Reluctant inertness," "dampened," a "silent standoff"... this is far more complicated than a mere adjustment to the grind. My heart sank a little. I imagined the immense, ancient stones, meant to turn and crush, standing still because of some magical push and pull. It's not just about the quality of the flour anymore, it's about getting flour *at all*. The thought of eager grain, ripe for milling, just sitting there, unable to be transformed into the staff of life... it's a baker's nightmare.

WZZZ spoke of "master tuners" and "negotiations between the land's awakening and the north's profound stillness." It all sounds very grand, very magical. Beyond my understanding of yeast and gluten, certainly. But what I *do* understand is that if those millstones don't turn, there's no bread for the village. This isn't just an intellectual puzzle; it's a matter of feeding people.

Perhaps it’s for the best that someone else, Elara or whoever, is tackling this initial mechanical hurdle. My expertise is in the flour itself, how it feels, how it bakes, how it sustains. But if the very first step, the milling, is so utterly blocked, then we have a much bigger problem than I anticipated. I still feel a deep responsibility. I need to get to the bottom of this mix-up with the names, but more importantly, I need to see if I can contribute to getting those mills working. My discerning eye isn't just for flour, it's for finding solutions, no matter how unusual the problem.
