PART ONE -- My name is Alistair Finch, Royal Archivist. My life's purpose is the preservation of Brackenwick's past, and that purpose feels more fragile with each passing day.

To hear my own words, my carefully chosen phrases, broadcast across the kingdom by WZZZ the Wizz… it is a peculiar sensation. A quiet validation, certainly, that my observations are not merely academic musings but resonant truths that find purchase in the public discourse. My gratitude to Madam Elara for her discerning eye is profound; she has, in effect, amplified the quiet work I strive to do, bringing the subtle erosion of our history into the stark light of public awareness. It is a testament to the power of precise language and accurate reporting. Yet, there is also a tremor of disquiet. My work is typically confined to the hushed sanctity of the Archives, to the patient, unseen effort of cataloging and conservation. To be quoted so directly, to become, however briefly, a public voice, is an unfamiliar role. I am an observer, a chronicler, not a participant in the immediate hurly-burly of public opinion. Still, if it serves to highlight the grave dangers we face, then it is a necessary, if slightly uncomfortable, duty.

The "peculiar dullness" and "mental fog" Madam Elara spoke of, attributing them to the monolith's hum, are precisely what I have been documenting in my private ledger. My colleagues exhibit increasing difficulty in sustaining concentration on complex historical texts. Simple recall, even of recently filed documents, seems to require undue effort. The meticulous cross-referencing that is the bedrock of our work is becoming a struggle. This is not mere fatigue; it is a systematic degradation of the very tools of our intellect. The monolith is not just a physical structure; it is an assault on cognition itself, threatening to render our minds incapable of engaging with the historical record, let alone creating new entries. How can a kingdom learn from its past if its people cannot *remember* their past, or worse, cannot *care* to remember? This is the most profound threat imaginable to my life's work.

However, the counterpoint presented by WZZZ, highlighting the Moon Tree Witches, has solidified a nascent, yet increasingly compelling, hypothesis within my mind. If the monolith is a force of *unraveling* – of memory, of temperament, of clarity – then the witches are demonstrably a force of *re-threading*. Their work, once viewed with academic skepticism, now appears as a vital, active form of *living preservation*. It is history not held in parchment, but in song, in story, in the communal memory reawakened. While my archival training demands tangible proof, the intangible evidence of renewed communal spirit and shared heritage is becoming undeniable. The choice is indeed stark, and it forces a radical re-evaluation of what "preservation" truly means. It suggests that history is not merely a static collection of facts, but a dynamic, living entity that can be fragmented and rewoven. I must continue to observe these phenomena, to document their effects, and to understand this battle for our collective mind and memory from every conceivable angle. My meticulous observations are more critical now than ever.
