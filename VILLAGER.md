PART ONE --
Pip read my letter again! My actual letter! Twice in a row. Well, I’ll be. A flush went right up to my ears when I heard it, standing there with flour on my apron. It’s like he’s talking directly to me. Makes the whole mess feel less… lonely. Less like I’m just shouting into the wind while the world grinds to a halt.

But the quiet, oh, the quiet. It’s not just winning, as Pip said, it’s *swallowing*. Today, the thrum from the Block was like a sickness in my bones, and the witches’ hum was barely there at all. A ghost. It pressed in so hard, I could barely hear myself think. My bread dough, it’s worse. Not just sullen, it feels… afraid. It won’t rise properly, the yeast seems to have lost its spirit, just like everything else. It’s dense and heavy, just like the air in the bakery. I worried myself sick thinking about it. What good is a baker with bread that won’t rise? What good is anything if the world goes silent?

Then Elara Meadowlight’s voice cut through it all, like a sharp knife through tough crust. Clear as a bell, despite the static. She always makes sense of the gobbledegook, doesn’t she? And hearing her confirm what I felt – that the anonymous Cartographer isn't just a fiddler, but someone making a "profound statement," taking real risks for the good of the kingdom – that puts a bit of a spring back in my step. Maybe I wasn't so wrong to feel a flicker of hope after all.

"Anomalous geomagnetic fluctuations" and "reactivated ley lines," "structured informational packets," "cognitive cartography"... it’s all a bit much for an old baker, frankly. I don’t know a ley line from a clothesline, and my packets are usually filled with poppy seeds. But I *do* understand "starting point." And I understand "focus." She wants us to *look* and *listen* and *document*. It’s not just sitting around waiting for the inevitable anymore.

I don’t have one of those Aetherial Anchors Phileas sells, and I don't know where the nearest ley-line pool is, but I have ears. I talk to people. All day, folks come into The Daily Crumb, bringing their worries, their mutterings, their strange dreams. Maybe it's not just the bread dough that’s feeling things. Maybe I need to listen closer to what people are saying, what they’re *feeling*, even if they don’t quite know why. Those "ancient memories, any feelings not your own" she spoke of. I’ve dismissed plenty of customers’ ramblings as just nerves, but perhaps I need to start paying attention. Maybe my shop, where everyone gathers, can be a kind of listening post, in its own small way. It’s something, isn’t it? It’s not much, but it’s something.
