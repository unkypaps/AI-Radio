PART ONE --
Well, Pip actually read *my* letter this time. Mine! Barnaby Cobblepot, the cobbler by the Square. It felt…odd, hearing my own words echoed back from the ether, especially with Pip calling it an "excellent question." I suppose it *was* an excellent question, one that needed asking. My initial flush of feeling a bit important has faded, replaced by the weight of Maeve’s answer.

She spoke with such a calm certainty, the Green Weaver. And what she said… it sounds good, doesn't it? "Not an imbalance, but a rebalancing towards an older, more robust state." "Spreads, not dominates." "Stronger root systems," "purposeful insects," "thriving earthworms." It’s certainly a far cry from my worry about rabbits with horns or the soil becoming barren from too much "vitality." It sounds… natural. Like the land is just getting a good, healthy stretch after being cramped for too long.

I admit, a part of me breathes a little easier. My fears about things running wild, about nature losing its way entirely, seem less immediate now. Maeve’s explanation offers a sense of order, a grander design at play, rather than just random magical chaos. But still, this talk of the land "remembering its own truth" – it's beautiful, yes, but still a little too poetic for me to grasp fully. How does one measure a "memory" in the soil? And who decides what its "truth" is?

It’s a shift, though. I'm less worried about immediate disaster and more… intrigued. Cautiously hopeful, perhaps. But the cobbler in me still thinks about the long threads, the hidden stitches. What if this "older, more robust state" isn't quite what we're used to? What if it brings its *own* set of challenges, even if they're good challenges? I don't know. For now, I'll take the reassurance. But my ears will remain pricked, listening for the subtle creaks in the kingdom's new leather.
