PART ONE -- He quoted me *again*! And he didn't just quote me, he used Old Man Thistle's story as "tangible clarity"! It makes me feel... important, in a way. Like my observations from the bakery are actually helping people understand what in the blazes is going on. He really does listen, and he explains things in a way that makes sense, even when the things themselves are utterly bewildering.

The distinction he made – that it's not just forgetting words, but the *meaning* slipping away – that clicks. It explains why the guards were stammering about "fish... no..." instead of completely forgetting how to speak. The *intent* behind the words, the *reason* for the regulation, simply wasn't there for them anymore. It's like trying to bake bread, but the concept of "rising" just... vanishes from your mind. You have flour and water, but no understanding of what they're supposed to *do*.

And he confirmed it! "Yes, Elara, I believe you will see tax collectors standing confused in the market." Well, if that isn't a silver lining on this whole obsidian cloud, I don't know what is. If the confusing, contradictory rules and taxes that burden the common folk are the first to simply lose their *meaning*, then perhaps this strange phenomenon isn't entirely bad. Imagine, no more arbitrary fines for the size of your turnip stall, or obscure taxes on the number of yeast bubbles in your sourdough! It could genuinely lighten the load for so many of my customers, and for me too.

He talks about the "kingdom itself" rejecting what is illogical and unsustainable, infused by the witches and the block. It's still a bizarre thought, this idea that the land itself has an opinion, but if its opinion is that confusing laws and unfair burdens are "discordant" and should melt away, then I'm certainly not going to argue with the kingdom's wisdom. My skepticism is still there, of course; what if it's *all* laws? What about the ones that keep order, that prevent thievery or violence? But for now, focusing on fishing regulations and taxes... that seems like a rather convenient and welcome side effect. Maybe there's more to this than just impending doom. Maybe the Wizz is right, maybe this *is* an acoustic assertion of truth. And perhaps, a quieter, less burdened Brackenwick for us all.
