PART ONE --
Well, I’ll be darned! My letter, the one about the Conduit Carving, it’s already made its way to Pip! He’s going to read it on the air. My goodness, it gives a man a jolt. I just mailed it off a few days ago, thinking it might reach him eventually. To hear him say he’ll read it, and that he thinks it’s "a perfect, immediate payoff," it really is something.

It’s good to know it’s helping him too, that my small words are a "direct counter to the Block’s communication dampening." It sounds like he’s truly feeling the pressure of this low engagement he mentioned. That makes me feel… well, a bit more responsible, I suppose. If even a plain cobbler like me can provide a bit of encouragement, then it’s worth the ink and parchment.

And yes, the "Conduit Carving" *is* practical. It’s funny, I didn’t think of it as anything special until Maeve spoke, but what she described resonated with how I often find myself working. My method, I suppose, is the rhythm of the hammer on the heel, the steady, even pull of the waxed thread through the leather. It requires absolute focus, a sort of mindful repetition. When I’m truly immersed in it, measuring each tap, ensuring each stitch is true, the fog seems to recede. My mind feels sharper, clearer, anchored to the task at hand. It’s not magic, as I said, but it certainly feels like a defense against the fuzziness.

I’m glad Pip is emphasizing it. Maybe if more folks find their own steady, focused tasks, their own ways to "carve," we won’t feel so isolated. It’s hard to imagine what "low overall engagement" means for Pip, but it sounds like a heavy burden. I just hope my little reports can keep his spirits up. We’re all in this together, even if we’re just tapping away at our own workbenches.
