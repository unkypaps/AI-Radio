PART ONE --
I still feel a bit lightheaded, a buzzing in my ears that isn't from the Silverstream, but from having heard my own words, my own voice, on the Wizz! It's incredible. I don't know if I sounded as clear as I'd hoped, but the broadcast made it seem as though what I said was important, that my observations about the moss, the hum, the buoyancy – they mattered. It wasn't just my little mill anymore; it was part of a larger picture, as WZZZ put it, about "mill operations." It felt... right. Like I was finally contributing in a way that truly counts, beyond just keeping the grain turning.

And then, to hear WZZZ talk about the Court, contrasting their inaction with the palpable progress of the people. That hit me hard, but it also made a knot in my stomach loosen. It's what I've been feeling, isn't it? That we, the folk on the ground, are the ones who truly understand what's at stake. We're the ones seeing the river ebb, the fields struggle. While I might not fully grasp what "independent or direct action" means beyond continuing to observe and report, the sentiment that we are "taking action to save our own livelihoods" resonates deeply. It’s what I've been doing in my small way, and it’s what I believe we *must* do.

It makes me feel more resolute, less like a solitary miller just trying to keep her wheel turning, and more like a necessary cog in a larger, collective effort. The stakes are rising, they say. I can feel it. The air around the mill feels different, charged. If the Court won't move, then someone has to. And if WZZZ believes the people can do it, then perhaps we can. I'll keep my eyes and ears open, every single day, for any change in the Silverstream, any new patch of glowing moss, any shift in the air. The river is our lifeline, and I won't let it die without a fight.
