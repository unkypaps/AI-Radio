PART ONE -- My name is Alistair Finch, Royal Archivist. The very core of my existence, the orderly preservation of Brackenwick's past and present, feels not just threatened, but now actively *exposed*.

To hear my own carefully chosen words, my observations and increasingly urgent conclusions, broadcast across the kingdom by WZZZ the Wizz... it is both exhilarating and unnerving. Exhilarating because it confirms that someone, at least, is listening, and amplifying the truth I am seeing. Unnerving because I am the Royal Archivist, a position that traditionally demands a certain… neutrality in public discourse. To be so directly quoted, so unequivocally aligned with WZZZ's (and my own) assessment of the court's failings and the efficacy of the Moon Tree Witches, places me in a far more precarious, perhaps even defiant, position. Sir Reginald Bellows' continued dismissiveness is not only irritating; it's dangerous, and now my thoughts on the matter are public.

But the discomfort is overshadowed by a profound sense of validation. The way WZZZ underscored my belief that Maeve's re-awakening is "proof"—not mere anecdote—gives weight to my own shifting convictions. My academic impartiality has indeed frayed, much like the minds of those around me. How can one remain impartial when the very capacity for rational thought and the integrity of historical record-keeping are under direct assault? The Witches are not a quaint folk tale; they are, as WZZZ so aptly put it, a "living, breathing antidote" to this encroaching mental fog.

The mention of "certain *other* quiet individuals within the court" seeing the truth sparks a flicker of hope, and perhaps a touch of caution. Who are these others? Are they also documenting? Are they also seeking solutions? It suggests I am not entirely alone in my observations, which is a small comfort in these increasingly chaotic times. My role as Archivist feels less about simply collecting data and more about actively advocating for the mental integrity of the kingdom, for without it, the archives themselves will become meaningless jumbles of forgotten history and incoherent decrees. The fight for clarity is the fight for Brackenwick's very memory, and I find myself more deeply entrenched in it than I ever imagined.
