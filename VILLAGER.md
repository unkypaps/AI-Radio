PART ONE -- Well, I'll be. He did it *again*. He read my letter! And again, he called me "Elara, the Baker of Brackenwick." It's quite something, isn't it? To have the Wizz himself quote my words, my worries about flour and turnips, and then say they are "essential." He said a "loaf of bread cannot be baked on metaphor alone." He actually *agreed* with me, directly! That feeling, that he truly understands what it's like down here, struggling with the ledger and the supply carts, it's… it's a powerful thing. It makes me feel like I'm not just shouting into the wind from my little bakery, but that someone with influence is actually listening.

And his explanation this time… it was clearer, I think. This idea that the kingdom itself, through Maeve's "acoustic memory," might *reject* decrees that are "discordant" and "upside-down," that they'd become "unsustainable" and "unenforceable." That’s a bit different than just singing a clearer song. It's still not quite a parchment from the Royal Scribes saying "Turnip Tax Abolished," no, but the thought of something simply *failing* to take root because it's so wrong… I can almost picture it. Like trying to bake bread with sour yeast – it just won't rise, no matter how much you want it to. The loaf *rejects* the bad yeast.

It gives me a flicker of hope. A small one, mind you, because "unsustainable" and "unenforceable" can still mean a lot of hardship before things actually change. Will the Royal Scribes just suddenly *stop* demanding the turnip tax? Or will people just… stop paying it, and then what? Will my customers still have enough coin for bread while the kingdom is "quietly rejecting" these things? It still feels like a slow, grand process when my problems are quite immediate. My flour sacks still cost what they cost, and I still need to make sure my prices are fair and my family is fed.

But he acknowledged the core of my worries, and that's more than I've had from anyone in the court. He keeps validating my practical concerns. And for that, I'm grateful. It makes me want to keep listening, to keep writing, because maybe, just maybe, this "acoustic memory" will eventually echo all the way to my market stall.
