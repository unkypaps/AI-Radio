PART ONE --
Oh, my dear journal, what a morning! My hands are still dusted with flour from the sourdough, but my mind is buzzing like the bees in the meadowsweet outside my shop. Barnaby Button Willowbrook, that’s me, of "The Sweet Hearth" bakery, established in Brackenwick for generations. My grandfather, old Silas Willowbrook, built this very oven, and my grandmother, Elara (named after *her*), taught me how to coax the perfect crumb from a simple loaf. Baking isn't just a job for me, it's a way of life, a daily rhythm, a comfort. I wake before dawn, the smell of rising dough is my perfume, and the chatter of my customers is my morning song. My greatest joy is seeing a child's face light up at one of my honey cakes, or a weary farmer take a hearty bite of my sourdough. My biggest worry? A bad batch of yeast, a shortage of good flour, or anything that disrupts the peaceful hum of my ovens and the happiness of my village.

And speaking of disruption… this monolith. And now the Witches. I won’t lie, Journal, it’s been a lot. At first, I was worried sick. Will the magic make my yeast go flat? Will my ovens suddenly bake stone instead of bread? Will people even *want* sweet treats if the sky starts falling? But Pip… Pip is a marvel, isn't he? He makes everything sound so hopeful, so important. And today, with Elara Meadowsweet talking about her gardening tools… well, that actually *made sense* to me.

I mean, I don't know much about "symbiotic reciprocity" or "resource extraction" in the grand scheme of things, but I *do* know tools. My grandmother’s rolling pin, worn smooth in places, feels entirely different from the new, machine-made one I bought when the old one finally cracked. There’s a certain heft, a certain *give* in the old one that just feels right, like it knows what the dough needs. And my copper sugar whisk? It’s seen a hundred thousand meringues, and it just *works*. I understand what she means about a "gentle hum" versus a "gnawing impatience." I’ve certainly felt the difference between carefully nurturing a sourdough starter and trying to rush a batch with too much commercial yeast. One leaves you with a vibrant, flavorful loaf; the other… well, it’s just bread.

The "point of fracture" she spoke of, that’s a bit big for my baker’s brain, but if it means we’re fixing things, moving towards "new growth" and "balance," then I’m all for it. A balanced Brackenwick is a happy Brackenwick, and happy villagers buy more honey cakes, don't they? And that dig at the Royal Court! Honestly, they spend more time polishing their tea-spoons than worrying about the price of flour. Pip’s right; we, the common folk, are the ones living and feeling it. I still have my worries, of course. My business depends on stability. But hearing Elara talk about tools, and the *care* put into them, it gives me a good feeling. Maybe these Witches aren't here to turn my gingerbread men into frogs after all. Maybe they're here to help us make everything a bit more… flavorful.
