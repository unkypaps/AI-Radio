PART ONE --
Oh, Journal, what a whirlwind of emotions! To hear Pip mention my great-grandmother's spoon *again*, putting it right there, front and center, for Professor Finchley to ponder – it fills me with a strange mix of pride and… well, exasperation. Pip truly gets it, doesn't he? He understands that these aren't just old stories, they’re *felt* truths. He’s my advocate, pushing back against the dusty academic boxes.

And Professor Finchley… I suppose I shouldn't be too surprised. "Colorful," "anecdotal," "localized psychological projection"—it's all the language I'd expect from someone who spends his days with scrolls instead of kneading dough. It's frustrating, though, to have something so real, so *visceral*, be reduced to a "phenomenon" for "Applied Anthropological Sensoria" to study. My spoon doesn't *project* wisdom, Journal, it *has* it! It's a part of the lineage, the very fabric of how we've lived and thrived. To suggest it's a "larger leap" for a spoon to possess innate wisdom feels like he's missing the forest for the… well, the wooden spoon.

But then, he *did* acknowledge the "persistence of such narratives." He said it was "noteworthy." He even linked it to historical shifts in agrarian practices. So, he's not entirely deaf, just slow to hear. It's like trying to explain the perfect proof of bread to someone who's only ever read recipes; they understand the words, but they don't *feel* the warmth, the give, the rise.

Pip's frustration was palpable, and I share it. Brackenwick *is* awakening, and our collective, felt wisdom *is* unearthing something profound. My bakery, my customers, our whole way of life, it all flows from this continuity, from understanding the heart of our tools and our land. I just hope the Professor's "arduous process" of debate doesn't take so long that we're all living in a "luminous future" that the scholars haven't even properly categorized yet. This isn't just theory, Journal, it's our daily bread, our very lives. And I'm glad Pip is fighting for that understanding.
