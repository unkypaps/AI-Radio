PART ONE -- Well, I'll be. They actually read my letter! My words, right there, on the air, for all of Brackenwick to hear. "Elara, the Baker of Brackenwick," he said. And he called me "beloved." It felt… it felt good, I must admit. For a moment, it felt like someone was truly listening, that the chaos might actually be heard and understood for what it is: a sticky mess. Wizz even agreed, "a stable foundation, free from 'interpretive vertigo' and baffling turnip tariffs, is essential for our kingdom to thrive." He said *my* words resonated deeply. It almost made me feel a glimmer of hope, like maybe, just maybe, things could start to make sense again.

But then… then he brought on Maeve again. And it was back to "acoustic memory" and "forgotten whispers" and the land "clearing its throat." Now, I'm not saying there's no truth to what Maeve says. I can appreciate a well-tended garden, and healthy soil is good for everyone. But what good is a "pure, unmistakable note" if the royal decrees are still playing an off-key lullaby about upside-down turnips? How does "vibrational levels" help me figure out if I'm paying triple for my flour next week, or if my customers can even afford to buy bread?

It's like I'm asking for a clear recipe for a loaf of bread, and Wizz acknowledges that, yes, a clear recipe is important, but then introduces a new ingredient that's all about "the spiritual potency of the wheat's forgotten ancestry" instead of, you know, just telling me how much yeast to add. It’s all very poetic, and I can see the appeal of things feeling "tuned" or "calmer," but it's still not the simple, practical clarity I need to run my bakery and feed my family.

The ground still feels like it's shifting, but perhaps, just perhaps, if enough of us speak up, Wizz will keep bringing those grounded truths to the forefront. It was good to hear my voice, even if the answers are still a bit too airy for my taste.
