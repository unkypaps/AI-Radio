PART ONE -- He called me out again, WZZZ did! My name, Elara, echoing across Brackenwick. It's a strange feeling, to have my practical worries given such public voice. And Maeve, bless her heart, she truly addressed my question. She didn't dismiss my fears about cold ovens; she acknowledged them, and then offered... a path.

"The Green Weaver's Restoration Kits." And it's not "magic" in the way I used to think of it, not chaotic or unpredictable. Maeve made it sound like listening, like remembering. "The land holding its breath, waiting," and the Witches "breathing out again." That imagery... it resonates. It makes sense in a way that pure "magic" never did for my practical mind. It’s not about fighting the Block, but aligning with something older, deeper.

And the crops! "Forgotten varieties of grain, root vegetables that haven't been cultivated in centuries, not just surviving but *flourishing*." This is what truly grips me. New kinds of flour? Imagine the possibilities! The different textures, the smells, the tastes! My mind is already buzzing with recipes, with how these ancient grains might behave in the dough, how they might rise. It's daunting, certainly, to think of adapting, but also incredibly exciting. A whole new world of bread to discover, if we can just get our hands on the grain.

The idea of "planting patterns that echo the witches' own map" still feels a bit... outside my direct experience. I work with flour, water, yeast, heat. Not ley lines. But if Maeve, a farmer, is seeing results, then who am I to doubt? My trust in the earth, in growth, is foundational. And if the earth is singing a new song, then perhaps I must learn to bake to its tune.

I'm not scared in the same way I was. The fear of utter barrenness, of my ovens lying cold forever, has receded a little, replaced by a new kind of challenge. It's still survival, but now it feels like survival through adaptation and discovery, rather than just clinging to the past. The biggest question now isn't *if* we can grow food, but *how* we, the bakers, the consumers, will access these new old ingredients. My hands are itching to work with these 'forgotten' grains.
