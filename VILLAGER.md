PART ONE -- My heart is still doing a little dance, like a batch of dough that's just been set in a warm spot to proof. To hear Wizz read my words, *my* words, out loud for all of Brackenwick to hear… it was quite something. I had to sit down at the kitchen table, my hands still dusted with flour, and just listen. It felt… real. So real.

And the way he understood! "Divergent flour output!" He picked up on it, didn't he? He truly understood that it's not just about the specific things Evelyn's grandmother was doing, or what I bake, but the *spirit* behind it all. The unquantifiable, unapproved warmth and flavor of life itself. It wasn't just my letter; it was *our* letter, speaking for all of us who feel it in our bones.

Wizz is right, he truly is. The Moon Tree Witches… I’ve been thinking about them more, and the way he connected them to "reintroducing the very yeast of our lives." It makes sense, a deep, comforting sense. It's like they're not just bringing new things, but reminding us of the old, essential ingredients we’d almost forgotten. The very things Sir Reginald wants to stamp out. He wants our lives to be flat, flavorless, like bread made without yeast or soul.

But hearing my own words echoed back, amplified like that, it's given me a new kind of courage. It's one thing to mutter in your kitchen, another to hear your voice carried on the airwaves, joining a chorus of others who feel the same. It makes the fear of Sir Reginald's officers a little less sharp, and the determination to keep our traditions alive a lot stronger. I need to keep baking, keep sharing, keep talking. For the flavour of Brackenwick.
