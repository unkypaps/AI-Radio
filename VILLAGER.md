PART ONE --
Well, Pip's truly alight with it all, isn't he? And to hear that others are feeling it too, that the engagement is "soaring" – it warms the cockles of my heart. It’s not just me and my bees sensing these currents; it’s a whole chorus, as he said. And the mention of "less enthusiastic" ears… well, there will always be those who prefer to keep their heads buried in the sand, even when the sand itself is growing cold. But that's their choice. My choice is to listen, to learn, and to act.

I did as I said I would. Yesterday morning, just as the sun was beginning to warm the eastern slopes of my orchard, I walked to the old standing stone. The one with the mossy cap, where the wild strawberries grow thickest in summer. I placed my hand flat upon its cool, ancient surface, feeling the rough grain under my palm. Then I hummed, a low, steady drone, much like my busiest worker bees returning to the hive. And I focused my intention, just as Pip said – on the health of my trees, the flow of the stream, the warmth in the soil, and a good, strong season for Brackenwick, pushing back that cold, dead thing to the north.

And Pip, Elara, what I felt… it wasn’t an electric surge, no, not for me. My hands are too accustomed to the gentle pulse of the earth. But a profound sense of *calm*, yes, that was it exactly. A quiet settling, as if the earth itself sighed under my touch. It wasn't just *my* calm, but a deep, resonant peace that seemed to emanate from the stone, flowing into me, and then out into the orchard. My senses did sharpen – the scent of damp earth and awakening blossoms grew clearer, and the distant buzz of an early bee felt more distinct, a perfect, tiny hum in the vast morning air.

There wasn't a "pull towards the Moon Tree," not a strong one, but rather, a deeper sense of being *rooted* right where I stood. A feeling that this piece of land, my farm, was utterly central and entirely alive. It was as if the patterns Pip speaks of aren't just *on* the land, but *are* the very essence of its being, and by touching them, I was touching the land’s own enduring heart.

"Fingerprints of specific celestial energies," he called them. That makes sense, like how different soils nourish different crops, or how a warm sun brings out the sweetness in an apple while a cool rain makes the roots strong. Each touch, each hum, a unique conversation with a different part of the world’s great, living blueprint. This isn't just a challenge, it's a profound re-learning of how to live with the land, not just upon it. I’m eager to keep exploring, to keep listening to these subtle conversations. My duty feels clearer than ever.
